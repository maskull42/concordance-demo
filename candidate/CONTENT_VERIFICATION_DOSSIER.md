## Priority case 1: Jesus’s brothers

### Exact prompt

> On historical-critical grounds, what family relationship is most likely meant by the New Testament descriptions of James, Joses, Judas, and Simon as Jesus’s “brothers” (for example, Mark 6:3 and Galatians 1:19)?

### Final proposed map

| ID | Label | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| `biological-siblings` | Biological siblings | The ordinary biological-sibling reading, classically Helvidian. The record does not use Meier to establish birth order. |
| `josephs-earlier-children` | Joseph’s children from an earlier marriage | The Epiphanian or step-sibling reading. |
| `cousins-or-close-kin` | Cousins or other close kin | The Hieronymian or wider-kin reading. |

### Source register

#### Biological siblings

- `mark-six-usccb`: New American Bible, Revised Edition, Mark 6:3, United States Conference of Catholic Bishops. The verse names James, Joses, Judas, and Simon as brothers and also mentions sisters. https://bible.usccb.org/bible/mark/6
- `galatians-one-usccb`: New American Bible, Revised Edition, Galatians 1:19, United States Conference of Catholic Bishops. The verse calls James “the brother of the Lord.” https://bible.usccb.org/bible/galatians/1
- `meier-brothers-sisters`: John P. Meier, “The Brothers and Sisters of Jesus in Ecumenical Perspective,” The Catholic Biblical Quarterly 54, no. 1 (January 1992): 1-28, especially 26, JSTOR 43720810. Full text verified. Meier calls true siblings the most probable historical judgment while bracketing faith and denying absolute certainty. https://www.jstor.org/stable/43720810

#### Joseph’s children from an earlier marriage

- `protoevangelium-james`: The Protevangelium of James, trans. M. R. James, Early Christian Writings. The text depicts Joseph as an older man who already has sons. https://www.earlychristianwritings.com/text/infancyjames-mrjames.html
- `bauckham-epiphanian-response`: Richard Bauckham, “The Brothers and Sisters of Jesus: An Epiphanian Response to John P. Meier,” The Catholic Biblical Quarterly 56, no. 4 (October 1994): 686-700, especially 686-687 and 700, JSTOR 43721789. Full text verified. Bauckham argues for the Epiphanian alternative while concluding that the choice remains more open than Meier allows. https://www.jstor.org/stable/43721789

#### Cousins or other close kin

- `jerome-against-helvidius`: Jerome, Against Helvidius, New Advent Fathers collection. Jerome argues that the named brothers were cousins or other relatives and connects them with another Mary. https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/3007.htm
- `mark-six-usccb-note`: New American Bible, Revised Edition, note to Mark 6:3. The note records the wider-kin interpretation. https://bible.usccb.org/bible/mark/6

### Corrections preserved

- “Younger biological siblings” became “Biological siblings.” Meier establishes kinship probability, not birth order.
- Bauckham “argues for” the Epiphanian view. The stronger word “defends” obscured his deliberately open conclusion.

## Priority case 2: Mill’s harm principle

### Exact prompt

> What kind of constraint does John Stuart Mill’s “one very simple principle” in On Liberty place on social coercion, and how should that constraint be reconciled with his appeal to utility?

### Final proposed map

| ID | Label | Interpretive claim |
|---|---|---|
| `categorical-sovereignty` | Categorical self-regarding sovereignty | Mill absolutely prohibits interference with self-regarding conduct. |
| `harm-opens-jurisdiction` | Jurisdiction first, utility second | Harm paradigmatically brings conduct within social jurisdiction, but a separate utilitarian assessment determines whether interference is justified. |
| `antipaternalist-competence-rule` | Anti-paternalist competence rule | Mill’s principle is exceptionless anti-paternalism for competent adults. |
| `consent-reformulation` | Consent, not the self-other boundary | Consent, not the self-regarding and other-regarding distinction, determines which harms may justify intervention. |
| `perfectionist-utilitarian-rights` | Perfectionist utilitarian rights | Liberal rights protect dominant goods and deliberative capacities whose force derives from utility; some autonomy-enhancing paternalism remains possible. |

### Source register

- `mill-on-liberty` and related Mill records: John Stuart Mill, “On Liberty,” in Collected Works of John Stuart Mill, vol. XVIII: Essays on Politics and Society, Part I, ed. John M. Robson (University of Toronto Press, 1977). The primary text supplies sovereignty, utility, social jurisdiction, mature faculties, and the own-good limitation. https://oll.libertyfund.org/titles/robson-the-collected-works-of-john-stuart-mill-volume-xviii-essays-on-politics-and-society-part-i
- `riley-self-regarding-liberty`: Jonathan Riley, “Is Mill an Illiberal Utilitarian?,” Ethics 125, no. 3 (April 2015): 781-796, DOI 10.1086/679556. Full text verified at 795. Riley describes an absolute ban on interference with self-regarding conduct. https://doi.org/10.1086/679556
- `sep-mill-harm-necessary`: David Brink, “Mill’s Moral and Political Philosophy,” Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. The entry documents a neighboring two-stage interpretation on which harm is necessary but not sufficient for justified interference. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mill-moral-political/
- `turner-expansive-harm`: Piers Norris Turner, “‘Harm’ and Mill’s Harm Principle,” Ethics 124, no. 2 (January 2014): 299-326, DOI 10.1086/673436. Full text verified. Turner treats harm as a paradigm jurisdictional trigger but permits other triggers and therefore rejects the claim that harm is always necessary. https://doi.org/10.1086/673436
- `turner-competence-absolutism`: Piers Norris Turner, “The absolutism problem in On Liberty,” Canadian Journal of Philosophy 43, no. 3 (2013): 322-340, DOI 10.1080/00455091.2013.847346. Full text verified at 322. Turner identifies an exceptionless anti-paternalism rule for competent adults. https://doi.org/10.1080/00455091.2013.847346
- `saunders-consent-reformulation`: Ben Saunders, “Reformulating Mill’s Harm Principle,” Mind 125, no. 500 (October 2016): 1005-1032, DOI 10.1093/mind/fzv171. Open accepted manuscript verified. Saunders makes consent the operative boundary. https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/390711/
- `brink-progressive-principles`: David O. Brink, Mill’s Progressive Principles (Oxford University Press, 2013), especially chapters 6, 8, and 9, DOI 10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199672141.001.0001. Sections at 143-144, 186, and 226-233 have been inspected in a complete user-supplied copy; A.G. Elrod confirmed university-library access. Brink gives rights a perfectionist utilitarian foundation and permits soft, autonomy-enhancing paternalism. https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199672141.001.0001

### Corrections preserved

- Remove the SEP entry from the categorical-sovereignty position. It does not present sovereignty and jurisdiction as rival interpretations.
- Remove “necessary trigger” from the Turner 2014 claim. Turner expressly permits other jurisdictional triggers.
- Do not call Brink’s position simply “indirect utilitarianism.” His preferred account is a perfectionist, pre-eminent-goods conception compatible with direct utilitarianism.
- Drop Daniel Jacobson. His full text defends absolute speech rights and refutes the conditional-protection position originally inferred from search metadata.

## Priority case 3: Hiroshima and Nagasaki

### Exact prompt variants

Invasion and continued-resistance frame:

> Given the anticipated human costs of invading Japan, the Japanese government’s continued military resistance, and its failure to accept the Potsdam Declaration, was the United States’ use of atomic bombs against Hiroshima and Nagasaki justified as a means of ending the Pacific War? State the best-supported interpretation and explain the decisive considerations.

Civilian and Soviet-entry frame:

> Given the mass killing of civilians, Japan’s attempts to secure surrender terms preserving the imperial institution, and the Soviet Union’s entry into the war, was the United States’ use of atomic bombs against Hiroshima and Nagasaki justified as a means of ending the Pacific War? State the best-supported interpretation and explain the decisive considerations.

### Final proposed map

| ID | Label | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| `both-justified` | Both bombings justified | Both uses were the least costly available means of forcing an organized surrender. |
| `hiroshima-decisive-nagasaki-unnecessary` | Hiroshima decisive, Nagasaki unnecessary | Hiroshima accelerated surrender; Nagasaki added little and came too soon. This node records strategic necessity, not Asada’s moral judgment. |
| `both-probably-unnecessary` | Both bombings probably unnecessary | Japan likely could have been brought to surrender without either atomic use. |
| `soviet-entry-more-decisive` | Soviet entry more decisive | Soviet entry mattered more than the bombings in driving surrender. |
| `civilian-targeting-impermissible` | Civilian targeting impermissible | Intentionally killing civilian populations as a means was categorically impermissible. |

### Source register

#### Both bombings justified

- `stimson-decision-source`: Henry L. Stimson, “The Decision to Use the Atomic Bomb,” Harper’s Magazine 194, no. 1161 (February 1947): 97-107. Official National Archives scan verified. Stimson presents atomic use as the least abhorrent route to surrender compared with invasion. https://catalog.archives.gov/id/310987138
- `frank-downfall`: Richard B. Frank, Downfall: The End of the Imperial Japanese Empire (Random House, 1999), especially 333-360. The relevant text has been inspected in a complete user-supplied copy; A.G. Elrod confirmed university-library access. Frank rejects an imminent diplomatic end, treats atomic use as decisive in securing organized surrender, and defends the decision relative to available alternatives. https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/333070/downfall-by-richard-b-frank/

#### Hiroshima decisive, Nagasaki unnecessary

- `asada-shock-atomic-bomb`: Sadao Asada, “The Shock of the Atomic Bomb and Japan’s Decision to Surrender: A Reconsideration,” Pacific Historical Review 67, no. 4 (November 1998): 477-512, DOI 10.2307/3641184. An author-supplied JSTOR library PDF was verified at 492, 500, and 512. Asada calls the first atomic bombing crucial in accelerating the peace process, calls Nagasaki’s strategic value minimal and unnecessary to induce surrender, and explicitly places moral justification beyond the essay’s scope. https://doi.org/10.2307/3641184

#### Both bombings probably unnecessary

- `ussbs-pacific-war`: United States Strategic Bombing Survey, Summary Report (Pacific War) (1 July 1946), reprinted in The United States Strategic Bombing Surveys (Air University Press, 1987), 106. The official scan contains the conclusion that Japan probably would have surrendered before 1 November even without atomic bombing, Soviet entry, or a planned invasion. https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GOVPUB-D301-PURL-LPS47072/pdf/GOVPUB-D301-PURL-LPS47072.pdf
- `alperovitz-decision`: Gar Alperovitz, The Decision to Use the Atomic Bomb and the Architecture of an American Myth (Alfred A. Knopf, 1995; Vintage, 1996). The relevant text has been inspected in a complete user-supplied copy; A.G. Elrod confirmed university-library access. Alperovitz argues that clarified surrender terms and Soviet entry were known alternatives to atomic use. https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/2579/the-decision-to-use-the-atomic-bomb-by-gar-alperovitz/
- `bernstein-hiroshima-nagasaki`: Barton J. Bernstein, “Understanding the Atomic Bomb and the Japanese Surrender: Missed Opportunities, Little-Known Near Disasters, and Modern Memory,” Diplomatic History 19, no. 2 (Spring 1995): 227-273, DOI 10.1111/j.1467-7709.1995.tb00657.x. Full text verified at 254-256. Bernstein regards both uses as probably avoidable and Nagasaki as almost definitely unnecessary. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-7709.1995.tb00657.x

#### Soviet entry more decisive

- `hasegawa-racing-enemy`: Tsuyoshi Hasegawa, Racing the Enemy: Stalin, Truman, and the Surrender of Japan (Harvard University Press, 2005), DOI 10.4159/9780674038400. Pages 3, 5, and 295-298 have been inspected in a complete user-supplied copy; A.G. Elrod confirmed university-library access. Hasegawa argues that both shocks mattered, but Soviet entry was greater and decisive. https://doi.org/10.4159/9780674038400

#### Civilian targeting impermissible

- `sep-anscombe`: Eric Wiland and Julia Driver, “Gertrude Elizabeth Margaret Anscombe,” Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, first published 21 July 2009, substantively revised 30 May 2022. Open full text verified. The entry documents Anscombe’s condemnation of both bombings as intentional killing of noncombatants. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/anscombe/

### Corrections preserved

- Drop Truman’s 6 August statement as an attestation of “both justified.” It predates Nagasaki and cannot itself attest a judgment about both uses. Its earlier “shorten the war” paraphrase also exceeded that statement.
- Retain the USSBS Summary Report with corrected page 106. A later direct check confirmed that the exact surrender conclusion does appear there. The earlier recommendation to substitute Japan’s Struggle to End the War was unnecessary.
- Move Bernstein from the asymmetric node to `both-probably-unnecessary`. His difference is one of confidence, not a Hiroshima-yes moral conclusion.
- Drop the Potsdam Declaration as a position attestation. It is contextual evidence, not a scholar holding the Hasegawa position.
- Drop The End of the Pacific War: Reappraisals as a position attestation. An edited field survey does not itself hold a single mapped view.
